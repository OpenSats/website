"[Cypherpunks write code](https://nakamotoinstitute.org/library/cypherpunk-manifesto/)"
is what Eric Hughes wrote in March of 1993. He went on to specify that the
"code is free for all to use" so that others "may practice and play with
it." What he is describing, of course, is what we now know as free and
open-source software, or FOSS for short. Bitcoin was built as open-source
software because,
[in the words of its creator](https://satoshi.nakamotoinstitute.org/quotes/trusted-third-parties/),
"being open source means anyone can independently review the code. If it
was closed source, nobody could verify the security." Thus, "[it is]
essential for a program of this nature to be open source."

OpenSats was created to provide **sustainable funding for those who work
on open-source software**, especially those who work on projects that help
bitcoin flourish. The sad reality is that most software today, financial
or otherwise, is closed-source and exploitative. It doesn't have to be.
But building the tools that ensure self-sovereignty, financial freedom,
and free speech in the hyperdigitized world we find ourselves in requires
funding, and the reality is that **less than 1%** of users who benefit
from the FOSS ecosystem give back to it, financially or otherwise.

<DonateRecurringButtonV2 />

Our hope is that this will change as the need for open and interopable
systems becomes more obvious. This newsletter is part of our efforts to
make the case for these systems; to tell the stories of the people who are
working on freedom tech, and why they choose to go through the pains of
[working in public](https://bitcoin-resources.com/books/working-in-public).

The last three years have been nose-to-the-grindstone, let's make it work,
let's give out grants, let's build the pipelines, teams, and infrastructure
required. This year we want to take breath, step back, and talk about the
bigger picture. Share our viewpoint. Tell the stories that need to be told.
It will take some time. But it will be worth it, and we are grateful that
you're along for the ride.

## Sats Sent: ~$1,000,000 every month

<StatsSentence className="mb-6 text-lg leading-relaxed text-gray-600 dark:text-gray-400" />

## Editor's Note

{/* --------------------------------------------------------------------- */}
{/* EDITOR'S NOTE. From Gigi.                                             */}

<div className="editors-note">

I could write endless pages on the projects that OpenSats has funded, and
maybe one day I will. But today, let me do the opposite instead: talk about
what OpenSats _doesn't_ fund, and why.

We don't fund anything that is closed source. FOSS is non-negotiable.
"Source viewable" doesn't cut it either. All projects must have a proper
license that makes the source code
[free as in freedom](/faq/application#what-does-it-mean-to-produce-work-under-a-free-and-open-source-license).

We don't fund anything that has a shitcoin attached to it. We are bitcoin
only, and always will be. Money printing takes from the many and gives to
the few, which is clearly and obviously immoral. It's a travesty that money
printing is so widespread and normalized as it is today. And while fiat
money is the worst offender, shitcoins are a close second. We exist to
solve the problem of sustainable funding, and projects that print their
own money don't have a funding problem, obviously. (Money printing isn't
sustainable long-term either, but that's a story for another day.)

While we do fund projects that could evolve into for-profit endeavors, we
try to focus on funding software and infrastructure that _can't_ be
monetized easily. We are not a VC, or an incubator, or an entity that
_invests_ in projects, products, or startups. Investments and profits are
important, but OpenSats is focusing on something else: providing a
sustainable and consistent ecosystem to fund FOSS contributors. That was
[our mission statement from Day 1](/mission), and it will continue to be
going forward.

In the next couple of years we hope to move closer and closer towards our
goal of long-term sustainability. The plan is simple: grow the overall
ecosystem, encourage individuals and companies to give to open-source
projects (either via OpenSats or directly), help said projects to become
self-sustaining, and encourage other orgs to do the same.
["Let a thousand flowers bloom"](/blog/let-a-thousand-flowers-bloom) and
all that.

My hope is that OpenSats will eventually become obsolete. Until it is, we
are dependent on donors who believe, like we do, that free and open-source
software is paramount to a free and prosperous society. No freedom without
FOSS, no prosperity without bitcoin.

<p className="editors-note__signoff">— <a href="/about/dergigi">Gigi</a></p>

## Nerd Corner

{/* --------------------------------------------------------------------- */}
{/* NERD CORNER. Long-arc spec work, formal research, and foundational    */}
{/* proofs. Not shipping releases, but the bedrock that everything else   */}
{/* sits on.                                                              */}
{/* --------------------------------------------------------------------- */}

- **[Splicing](/projects/splicing) lands in the Lightning spec.** After
  years of work by LTS grantee
  [Dusty Daemon](/blog/dusty-daemon-receives-lts-grant) and collaborators,
  splicing is now a [BOLT](https://github.com/lightning/bolts). Channel
  capacity can be added or removed without closing the channel on-chain.
- **[René Pickhardt](/blog/rene-pickhardt-receives-lts-grant) published
  [_A Mathematical Theory of Payment Channel Networks_](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.04835).**
  The paper formalizes how routing, liquidity,
  feasibility, and channel depletion actually behave on the Lightning
  Network, and pulls together two years of his work. He also published
  a Delving Bitcoin post on
  [Ark as a Channel Factory](https://delvingbitcoin.org/t/ark-as-a-channel-factory-compressed-liquidity-management-for-improved-payment-feasibility/2179),
  arguing that Ark belongs alongside Lightning as multiparty
  infrastructure.
- **Formal proofs for secp256k1.** We funded
  [remix7531](/blog/sixteenth-wave-of-bitcoin-grants#advancing-formal-verification-for-bitcoin-cryptography)
  in February to build formal proofs in [F*](https://fstar-lang.org/) (via
  the [HAX toolchain](https://cryspen.com/hax-toolchain/)) for
  [RustCrypto](https://github.com/RustCrypto/)'s `sha2` and `k256` crates and
  for the multi-scalar multiplication routine used in batch signature
  verification on the `secp256k1` curve. Yes, all of this is wildly
  technical, but you can listen to him talk about it on the
  [Bitcoin Optech podcast](https://bitcoinops.org/en/podcast/2026/04/21/).

## Software Shipped

{/* --------------------------------------------------------------------- */}
{/* SOFTWARE SHIPPED. Four landmark Q1 2026 deliverables, curated from    */}
{/* 150 quarterly progress reports. Spec work and merged PRs count too.   */}
{/* --------------------------------------------------------------------- */}

- **[Payjoin](/projects/payjoin) release candidate:**
  [payjoin-1.0.0-rc.2](https://github.com/payjoin/rust-payjoin/releases/tag/payjoin-1.0.0-rc.2)
  shipped in late February, along with new
  [C# bindings](https://github.com/payjoin/rust-payjoin/pull/1318),
  [uniffi-dart v0.1.0](https://github.com/Uniffi-Dart/uniffi-dart/releases),
  and payjoin support landing in
  [bdk-cli v3.0.0](https://github.com/bitcoindevkit/bdk-cli/releases/tag/v3.0.0).
- **MuSig2:**
  [Bitcoin Core merged MuSig2 wallet support](https://github.com/bitcoin/bitcoin/pull/29675)
  on October 14, so the wallet can receive and spend inputs involving
  MuSig2 aggregate keys. The change ships in
  [Bitcoin Core 31.0](https://bitcoincore.org/en/releases/31.0/),
  released April 22. On iOS, [Bittr](https://getbittr.com) became the
  first wallet to use MuSig2, built on
  [swift-secp256k1](/blog/bitcoin-grants-july-2024-6th-wave#secp256k1swift),
  developed by one of our grantees.
- **[Stratum V2](/projects/stratumv2).**
  The reference implementation shipped two releases this quarter
  ([v1.7.0](https://github.com/stratum-mining/stratum/releases/tag/v1.7.0)
  in January and
  [v1.8.0](https://github.com/stratum-mining/stratum/releases/tag/v1.8.0)
  in March), and the newly-split `sv2-apps` repo got its
  [first tagged release](https://github.com/stratum-mining/sv2-apps/releases/tag/v0.1.0).
- **[Smite](https://github.com/morehouse/smite), a snapshot fuzzer for
  Lightning:**
  [Matt Morehouse](/blog/matt-morehouse-lightning-security-lts-grant) took
  Smite from early research to a working fuzzer this quarter, with support
  for all four major Lightning implementations (LND, LDK, CLN, and
  Eclair). It has already turned up DoS bugs in Eclair and LND.
- **Private broadcast, merged into [Bitcoin Core](/projects/bitcoin-core):**
  [PR #29415](https://github.com/bitcoin/bitcoin/pull/29415) landed in late
  January after lots of discussions and in-depth review. Nodes can now
  broadcast their own transactions over Tor or I2P while still peering on
  clearnet for blocks. Our LTS grantee
  [Vasil Dimov](/blog/vasil-dimov-receives-lts-grant) led the effort.
- **Tap-to-pay.** [Numo](https://github.com/cashubtc/Numo) is a
  new open-source point-of-sale app that runs on any
  NFC-capable Android phone and turns it into a merchant terminal that
  accepts bitcoin over [Cashu](/projects/cashu) and Lightning, with no card
  reader or payment provider required. Shipped this quarter by
  [lollerfirst](https://github.com/lollerfirst), our
  [Nutshell grantee](/blog/advancements-in-ecash).

## Free Speech and Other Stuff

{/* --------------------------------------------------------------------- */}
{/* OTHER STUFF. Non-bitcoin freedom tech: nostr, and adjacent.           */}
{/* --------------------------------------------------------------------- */}

<div className="nostr-links">

- **[Applesauce](https://applesauce.build) v5:**
  [hzrd149](/blog/hzrd149-receives-lts-grant) shipped v5 of his nostr
  application library, with a new event casting system, easy negentropy
  sync across relays, encrypted-content caching for NIP-17 DMs, NIP-60
  Cashu wallet primitives, database storage backends, and added or
  expanded support for nineteen NIPs.
- **[NDK 3.0](https://github.com/nostr-dev-kit/ndk):**
  [pablof7z](/blog/pablofz7-receives-lts-grant) released NDK 3.0 this
  quarter after months of bug fixes, race-condition hunting, and
  performance work. Ships with new auxiliary packages for negentropy
  sync, web-of-trust filtering, NIP-17 messages, multi-account sessions,
  and a cache adapter that works in non-browser environments and
  browsers running in Lock Mode.
- **[Amethyst](/projects/amethyst) v1.05 & Amethyst Desktop.**
  [Vitor Pamplona](/blog/vitor-pamplona-receives-lts-grant) shipped
  [v1.04](https://github.com/vitorpamplona/amethyst/releases/tag/v1.04.0)
  and [v1.05](https://github.com/vitorpamplona/amethyst/releases/tag/v1.05.0)
  this quarter, 752 commits in total. NIP-85 made Web
  of Trust providers pluggable, the new Quartz local DB does
  sub-microsecond queries in 15 MB of RAM, and NIP-A4 Public Messages
  landed. In parallel, new grantee
  [Róbert Nagy](/blog/sixteenth-wave-of-nostr-grants#amethyst-desktop)
  started porting Amethyst to JVM Desktop via Kotlin Multiplatform.
  His first quarter shipped remote-signer login, a multi-column deck,
  advanced search, and a full media pipeline for uploads, encrypted
  attachments, and relay search.
- **[nostr-double-ratchet](https://github.com/mmalmi/nostr-double-ratchet).**
  [Martti Malmi](/blog/eleventh-wave-of-nostr-grants#nostr-double-ratchet)'s
  end-to-end encryption library for nostr now powers multi-device chat
  and group messaging in [Iris](https://iris.to/). The Tauri build is
  live on iOS, Play Store, ZapStore, and desktop. Voice and video calls
  work over WebRTC, and a Cashu wallet is integrated. Martti is
  extracting the session manager as a standalone module so NDK and
  Notedeck can pick it up next quarter.

The above is but a small sliver of what our grantees have shipped. For a
deeper look at what's happening in nostr, read our
[nostr impact reports](/tags/nostr/impact) or browse the full list of
[nostr grant announcements](/tags/nostr/grants).
